# Mapping the air we breathe

Every year, poor air quality contributes to tens of thousands of premature deaths in the UK. It causes respiratory and cardiovascular disease, affects children's development, and shortens lives. Yet for most people, the air around them remains invisible and unmeasured.

We know that air quality varies dramatically from street to street, and even from one side of a road to the other. But our ability to measure that variation is severely limited. Traditional air quality monitoring relies on fixed sensors mounted on lamp posts or buildings and there simply aren't enough of them to tell us what's really going on in the places where people actually live, work, and travel.

**The LocalAir Project is changing that.**

---

## The problem with static sensors

Fixed air quality sensors can be expensive to install and even more expensive to run. Each one needs a power supply, a communications link, and ongoing maintenance. The result is that most cities have only a handful of monitoring stations. This might be enough to give a rough city-wide picture, but nowhere near enough to understand what's happening on your street, outside your child's school, or along your daily commute.

What we need is a sensor network that can be deployed at scale, without the cost and complexity of fixed infrastructure. One that moves through the city continuously, gathering data from thousands of locations rather than just a handful.

---

## A novel solution: sensors on e-scooters

Electric scooters are already out there on the streets. They have large batteries that provide a reliable power source, they are connected to the internet, and they are constantly travelling to almost every corner of the city, not limited to roads but also through parks and along cycle paths. They are, in other words, the perfect mobile sensor platform.

By integrating bespoke air quality sensors directly into commercial e-scooter hardware, LocalAir turns an e-scooter into a roving environmental monitoring station. No fixed infrastructure required. No power cables. No communications masts. Just a city-wide network of sensors, gathering data wherever the scooters go.

The approach has real advantages over anything that has come before:

- **Scale** — a fleet of 100 sensor-equipped scooters covers more ground in a day than a static network of thousands of fixed sensors
- **Cost** — at around £300 per unit, the sensors are a fraction of the cost of traditional monitoring equipment
- **Flexibility** — sensors move with the fleet, automatically providing coverage wherever scooters are being ridden
- **Real-world relevance** — the data is gathered at street level, where people actually breathe, not metres above the ground on a lamp post

---

## Partnering with WECA and e-scooter operators

Local Air is a collaboration between the University of Bristol, the West of England Combined Authority (WECA), and commercial e-scooter operators. Together, we have designed, built, and deployed a sensor capable of integrating directly into commercial e-scooter hardware. We have already demonstrated the approach in a real-world deployment in London.

The project is now seeking funding to scale up to a full city-wide deployment in Bristol, generating three years of continuous, high-resolution air quality data that will directly inform local policy and public health decision-making.
